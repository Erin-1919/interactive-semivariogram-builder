A Quick Review

Spatial interpolation

The process of predicting unknown values at unsampled locations based on measurements from known locations.

Deterministic interpolation estimates unknown values using mathematical rules based on nearby observations, relying on distance or smoothness assumptions.

Common methods
  • Nearest Neighbor
  • Bilinear
  • Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW) — Our baseline method
* IDW key assumption: distance alone determines similarity

Why distance alone is not enough

What is Kriging?

A method born from mining geostatistics

Origins: Named after Danie G. Krige, a South African mining engineer in the 1950s who developed it to estimate gold ore grades from sparse samples.

Where IDW asks how far away each point is, Kriging asks how spatially related they are, how similar they are statistically, and how weights should be optimized to minimize prediction error.

Kriging as BLUP

Best Linear Unbiased Prediction

Kriging minimizes prediction error variance. Among all possible linear unbiased estimators, kriging gives the most precise estimate.

Best

Minimizes prediction error variance.

Linear

Prediction is a weighted sum of observed values, so Kriging remains mathematically interpretable.

Unbiased

Expected prediction error is zero — Kriging is statistically fair on average.

Ordinary Kriging — Core Assumption

Unknown but constant local mean

Ordinary Kriging assumes that within the local neighborhood of prediction, the underlying mean is constant but unknown.

Z(s) = μ + ε(s)
Z(s) — observed value at location s
μ — unknown but locally constant mean
ε(s) — spatially correlated random component

Ordinary Kriging assumes local stability without requiring global uniformity. It avoids the unrealistic assumption of Simple Kriging (known global mean), while also avoiding the complexity of Universal Kriging (explicit trend modeling).

When Ordinary Kriging is appropriate

A. Spatial autocorrelation exists

Nearby points should be more similar than distant points.

B. No strong large-scale trend

A major directional trend (e.g., temperature decreasing steadily with altitude) will make Ordinary Kriging underperform.

C. Sufficient sample density

A meaningful variogram requires enough data to estimate spatial dependence reliably.

D. Continuous variables

Designed for continuous numeric variables (rainfall, temperature, soil moisture).

Step 1 — Data exploration

Before kriging, thoroughly inspect the data to verify its assumptions.

Question Why it matters

Are there outliers? Outliers can distort the variogram
Is the distribution highly skewed? Transformation may be needed
Is there a spatial trend? Strong trend may require universal kriging

Step 2 — Empirical semivariogram

If two locations are close, their values should usually be more similar — lower semivariance. As distance increases, values become less similar, and semivariance tends to increase.

That relationship becomes the statistical foundation for how Kriging assigns weights.

Anatomy of an empirical semivariogram

Nugget

Value at or near origin (h=0). Represents measurement error or microscale variation.

Range

Distance where the curve levels off. Beyond this distance, points are effectively uncorrelated.

Sill

Plateau value. Approximate total variance of the data.

Step 3 — Fit a theoretical semivariogram model

An empirical semivariogram is a scatter of observed γ values — for Kriging we need a smooth, mathematically defined curve.

Spherical
Best used when
  • Spatial similarity decreases steadily then stops beyond a certain distance
  • General-purpose environmental data
Exponential
Best used when
  • Nearby points may differ substantially
  • Patchy or irregular variables
  • Air pollution, contaminant spread
Gaussian
Best used when
  • Spatial processes change smoothly over space
  • Highly continuous surfaces
  • Temperature, groundwater surfaces

Step 6 — Validation

Evaluate whether the kriging model performs well. A common approach is leave-one-out cross-validation:

Steps
  1. Remove one observed point
  2. Predict its value using the remaining points
  3. Compare predicted vs. observed value
  4. Repeat for all points

Different types of Kriging

Type Mean assumption Best for
Simple Kriging Known constant Rare theoretical cases
Ordinary Kriging ★ Unknown local constant Most common interpolation
Universal Kriging Trend / drift present Large-scale gradients
Indicator Kriging Binary or threshold probabilities Probability mapping
Co-Kriging Multiple correlated variables Multi-source prediction
