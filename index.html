A Quick Review

Spatial interpolation is the process of predicting unknown values at unsampled locations based on measurements from known locations.

Deterministic interpolation estimates unknown values using mathematical rules based on nearby observations, relying on distance or smoothness assumptions.

Common Methods:
  • Nearest Neighbor
  • Bilinear
  • Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW) - Our Baseline Method
* IDW key assumption: distance alone determines similarity

Why distance alone is not enough

Four scenarios, one lesson: two points the same distance apart can have wildly different values once you account for what is between them.

What Is Kriging?

Kriging is a geostatistical interpolation method that predicts unknown values using weighted nearby observations, where weights are determined by the spatial autocorrelation structure of the data.

General Formula:
Ẑ(s0) = n Σ i=1 λi Z(si)
Hover any term in the formula to highlight it
Key Difference from IDW:

IDW: Weights depend mainly on distance

Kriging: Weights depend on distance, spatial autocorrelation, spatial arrangement, and statistical optimization

Origins: Named after Danie G. Krige, a South African mining engineer in the 1950s.
Ordinary Kriging — Core Assumption

Unknown but constant local mean

Ordinary Kriging assumes that within the local neighborhood of prediction, the underlying mean is constant but unknown.

Z(s) = μ + ε(s)
Hover any term in the formula to highlight it
Further reading: Universal Kriging, Indicator Kriging, Co-Kriging

When Ordinary Kriging is appropriate

A. Spatial autocorrelation exists

Nearby points should be more similar than distant points.

B. No strong large-scale trend

A major directional trend (e.g., temperature decreasing steadily with altitude) will make Ordinary Kriging underperform.

C. Sufficient sample density

A meaningful variogram requires enough data to estimate spatial dependence reliably.

D. Continuous variables

Designed for continuous numeric variables (rainfall, temperature, soil moisture).

Step 2. Empirical Semivariogram Calculation

The semivariogram measures half the average squared difference between paired data values separated by distance h:

γ(h) = 1 2N(h) N(h) Σ i=1 [Z(xi) − Z(xi + h)]2
Hover any term in the formula to highlight it

In short: On average, how much do values differ when they are separated by distance h?

The spatial relationship becomes the statistical foundation for how Kriging assigns weights.
